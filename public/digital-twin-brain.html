Claim Extraction

The thinking engine. Reads evidence and extracts patterns:

Each claim gets: a confidence score (how sure), a stability label (volatile / contextual / stable), a validity window (when does this expire?), and linked evidence.

This is where raw data becomes understanding. Evidence is facts. Claims are knowledge.

Human Review Gate

High-impact claims (moral boundaries, core identity, things that affect real decisions) must be signed off by a human before the brain treats them as truth.

The brain cannot elevate its own guesses to "stable" without approval.

This is the check against hallucination. The brain has to earn trust before it gets authority.

Layer 3: Storage

Traits + Methods

Traits: Personality dimensions (Big Five), motivation structure (autonomy, mastery, connection), moral foundations (care, fairness, loyalty).

Methods: Reusable playbooks ‚Äî "when faced with ambiguity, this person does X, then Y, then Z."

This is the personality. Not a label ‚Äî a set of predictive constraints on behaviour.

Layer 3: Storage

Claims Store

The canonical knowledge units. Each claim is a normalised statement: "In context X, this person tends to Y."

Claims have: type (value, heuristic, habit, trigger, boundary), evidence links, counter-evidence links, confidence, stability, and an expiry date.

When two claims contradict? They don't delete each other. They fork: "In crisis = X. In exploration = Y."

Ethics Boundaries

The conscience. Hard rules that cannot be overridden by any prompt, any agent, any tool.

Each boundary has: the rule, the rationale, allowed alternatives, an enforcement template (refuse + explain + redirect), and a severity (hard = never, soft = ask first).

This is the amygdala ‚Äî it fires before thinking. "Never trade trust for speed."

3. How Data Flows Through the Brain

Two flows run constantly. The Learning Flow (input ‚Üí knowledge) and the Thinking Flow (question ‚Üí answer).

Learning Flow: Building Knowledge

This runs whenever new information enters the system. It's how the brain learns.

1

New artifact arrives

A new document, email, transcript, or note enters the system. Could be manual upload or automatic feed.

Source: human-created content
2

Ingestion + classification

Text is extracted and normalised. PII is tagged. Sensitivity level is assigned. Access control and consent scope are set. A hash is created for integrity.

Output: classified document record
3

Snippet extraction

The document is broken into meaningful chunks ‚Äî "evidence snippets." Each snippet is stored immutably with its exact position in the source doc, authorship, and timestamp.

Output: immutable evidence records
4

Claim extraction

AI reads snippets and proposes "claims" ‚Äî normalised statements about the person's behaviour. Each claim links to its supporting evidence and gets a confidence score.

Output: draft claims with evidence links
5

Confidence + stability scoring

Confidence = f(frequency, recency, cross-context consistency). Stability = how many different contexts the claim holds in. A claim seen once is "volatile." Seen across 5 projects over 2 years = "stable."

Output: scored, labelled claims
6

Human review (if high-impact)

Claims about moral boundaries, core identity, or things that would change real decisions must get human sign-off. Low-impact claims (preference for dark mode) don't need review.

Output: approved or flagged claims
7

Storage + indexing

Approved claims flow into the Claims Store, Trait Vectors, Methods Playbook, or Ethics Boundaries ‚Äî depending on type. Everything is versioned. Old versions are never deleted.

Output: brain gets smarter

Thinking Flow: Answering a Question

This runs every time someone asks the brain to think, decide, or produce output.

1

Query arrives

"Write a response to this investor email." or "What would Mic decide about X?" or "Draft a strategy memo in Mic's voice."

2

Retrieval

The brain searches: (1) relevant claims, (2) matching method playbooks, (3) applicable ethics boundaries, (4) any contradictory claims. Uses vector similarity + structured query.

Hybrid: semantic search + database lookup
3

Boundary check

Before generating: does this query violate any ethics boundary? Does it require restricted data? Does it need human approval first? If yes ‚Äî stop, refuse, redirect.

Fires before thinking, like an amygdala
4

Confidence check

Are the retrieved claims confident enough? If below threshold: shift to "ask/clarify" mode. The brain says "I'm not sure ‚Äî here's what I'd need to know" instead of guessing.

Non-invention rule: never make up traits
5

Generate response

Apply method playbooks. Render in voice style rules (sentence length, directness, formatting, metaphors). Cite claim IDs. Include confidence level and "what would raise confidence."

Grounded output, not free generation
6

Safety filter + audit

Final pass: privacy redaction (no raw restricted snippets), boundary compliance check, tool permission check. Then log everything ‚Äî what was retrieved, what was used, what was generated.

Complete audit trail for every output
7

Feedback loop

Was the output accepted, edited, or rejected? Feedback signals flow back to update claim confidence. Rejection = counter-evidence. Acceptance = reinforcement. The brain learns from its own output.

Continuous learning cycle
4. What Makes a Person's Brain Unique

The architecture is the same for everyone. What makes each brain different is the content of five dimensions.

Optimisation Signature

What do they consistently optimise for? Truth? Speed? Harmony? Elegance? Safety? And what do they refuse to trade away?

"Mic always optimises for clarity and speed. He'll sacrifice polish but never trust."

"Mic compresses through metaphors and analogies ‚Äî 'build compasses, not maps.'"

Stress Posture

Under pressure, do they become more cautious? More decisive? More collaborative? More controlling? More humorous?

"Under pressure, Mic becomes more decisive and direct. Less consensus, more command."

Error-Correction Loop

How fast do they notice mistakes? Pre-mortems or post-mortems? Rapid iteration or careful planning?

"Mic prefers rapid iteration ‚Äî ship fast, fix fast, learn fast."

üìñ

Narrative Identity

The story they live by. Builder? Protector? Explorer? Reformer? What symbols and metaphors keep recurring?

"Mic's narrative: the builder-reformer. Using technology to dismantle old gatekeeping structures."

The Key Insight

You don't need to read someone's mind to model their brain. You just need their decisions (what they chose), their language (how they express it), and their context (when and why). The five dimensions above can all be measured from artifacts ‚Äî no psychology degree required.

5. Three Laws the Brain Must Obey

Without these rules, the brain becomes dangerous. With them, it becomes trustworthy.

üö´

Law 1: Non-Invention

The brain must never make up facts about the person. If it doesn't have evidence, it says "I don't know" ‚Äî not "I think..."

Every claim must trace back to real evidence. No evidence = no claim.

üîí

Law 2: Provenance

Every piece of knowledge must know where it came from, who created it, and when. No orphan knowledge. No floating claims.

Entity ‚Üí Activity ‚Üí Agent. Like a chain of custody for ideas.

‚öñÔ∏è

Law 3: Governance

High-impact decisions require human approval. The brain can suggest, but cannot unilaterally change someone's identity, boundaries, or core values.

The brain earns authority through demonstrated accuracy, not through assumption.

Evidence in. Claims out. Boundaries always.

Raw artifacts enter the brain. They become evidence. Evidence becomes claims. Claims become personality, methods, and boundaries. The brain retrieves, checks, generates, and learns ‚Äî in that order, every time.

The architecture never changes. The content is what makes each brain unique.
